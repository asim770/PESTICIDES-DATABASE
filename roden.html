Rodenticides

Rodenticides are chemical substances or compounds used to control and eliminate rodent populations. Rodents, including rats and mice, are notorious for spreading diseases, contaminating food supplies, and damaging property by gnawing on wires, insulation, and buildings. Rodenticides are widely used in both urban and rural areas to mitigate the damage caused by rodent infestations, as well as in agriculture to protect crops.The global rodenticide market is valued at $1.7 billion USD in 2023 and is projected to grow at a CAGR of 6% over the next few years, reaching over $2 billion by 2030.The demand for rodenticides is driven by the need to manage rodents in agriculture, urban areas, and industrial settings.

Common examples of rodenticides:

Rodenticides are generally classified into acute and chronic types based on how quickly they affect the rodent after consumption. Some common examples of rodenticides include:

  1. Anticoagulants (Chronic Rodenticides):

    These work by interfering with blood clotting, causing the rodent to bleed internally, eventually leading to death. These are the most widely used rodenticides and are often available over-the-counter.
    • Brodifacoum: A highly toxic anticoagulant used in rodent control. It can be lethal to rodents after a single dose and is commonly used in commercial and residential areas.
    • Warfarin: One of the first anticoagulant rodenticides. It is less toxic than newer alternatives but still effective. Warfarin is often used in agricultural settings.
    • Difenacoum: Similar to Brodifacoum but less persistent in the environment.
    • Clorophacinone: A less toxic anticoagulant compared to Brodifacoum but still widely used.

  2. Non-Anticoagulants (Acute Rodenticides):

    These cause death by disrupting the rodent's central nervous system or metabolic processes.
    • Zinc Phosphide: A toxic rodenticide that releases phosphine gas when it comes into contact with stomach acid, causing immediate death. It is used in agricultural settings, particularly for crops like wheat.
    • Bromethalin: A neurotoxic rodenticide that disrupts cellular energy production. It is highly toxic to rodents and is used in both residential and commercial pest control.
    • Cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3): Causes hypercalcemia (high calcium levels), leading to organ failure and death. It is often marketed as a "humane" rodenticide because it acts more slowly than anticoagulants, but it can still be toxic to non-target animals.

Uses of Rodenticides

Rodenticides are used in various settings to manage rodent populations, including:

  1. Agriculture: Rodenticides are widely used on farms to prevent rodents from damaging crops, storing grain, or contaminating food supplies. Rodents like field mice and voles can cause extensive damage to crops such as corn, wheat, and soybeans.
  2. Urban Pest Control: In cities and towns, rodenticides are used in homes, commercial buildings, sewers, and trash disposal areas to control rat and mouse populations. In urban areas, rats often thrive in environments where food waste and shelter are abundant.
  3. Warehouses and Food Storage: Rodenticides are used in warehouses and storage facilities, particularly those storing food products. Mice and rats are known to contaminate food and cause significant financial losses in the retail and food production industries.
  4. Public Health: Rodent control is critical in preventing the spread of diseases like Leptospirosis, Salmonella, and Hantavirus, which rodents can carry and transmit to humans. Rodenticides are part of public health efforts to reduce the risks posed by urban rodent populations.
  5. Home Gardens and Yards: Some homeowners use rodenticides to manage rodent infestations in their gardens or yards, especially if rodents are eating their plants or burrowing around structures.

Harms of Rodenticides

While rodenticides are effective at controlling rodent populations, they pose significant risks to human health, pets, wildlife, and the environment. The harms of rodenticides can include

  1. Risks to Non-Target Animals (Wildlife and Pets)

    • Secondary Poisoning: When rodents ingest rodenticides and are then eaten by predators like owls, hawks, foxes, or even pets, these animals can suffer from secondary poisoning. For example, Brodifacoum and other anticoagulant rodenticides can remain in the tissue of rodents for weeks, causing harm to animals that consume them. In California, studies have shown that hawks, eagles, and mountain lions have been poisoned by eating rodents that consumed anticoagulant rodenticides.
    • Pets: Dogs and cats can be accidentally poisoned if they ingest rodenticide bait or rodents that have consumed the poison. Pets may display symptoms such as lethargy, bleeding, or seizures. In the U.S., the Pet Poison Helpline receives hundreds of calls annually about rodenticide exposure.

  2. Environmental Impact

    • Contamination of Soil and Water: Rodenticides can contaminate the soil and water if improperly applied. For example, Zinc Phosphide can release toxic phosphine gas, which poses a danger to the surrounding environment, including plants and animals.
    • Biodiversity Loss: The widespread use of rodenticides, especially in agricultural areas, has been linked to declines in predator populations, particularly birds of prey. In the U.S., barn owls and red-tailed hawks have been found to have elevated levels of rodenticide toxins in their systems due to consuming contaminated rodents.

  3. Human Health Risks

    • Acute Poisoning: Accidental exposure to rodenticides can cause poisoning in humans. For instance, exposure to warfarin and brodifacoum can lead to internal bleeding and hemorrhaging, requiring immediate medical attention.
    • Long-Term Health Effects: Chronic exposure to rodenticides can potentially affect the liver, kidneys, and reproductive system, although these effects are more often associated with high-level exposure.

  4. Resistance and Overuse

    • Resistance: Over time, rodent populations can develop resistance to certain rodenticides, particularly anticoagulants. This means that higher doses or alternative chemicals may need to be used, which can increase environmental and health risks.
    • Overuse and Misapplication: Over-reliance on rodenticides can lead to ineffective control and long-term environmental damage. For instance, improperly applied rodenticides can persist in the environment and continue to harm non-target species.
